ON THE IDENTIFICATION OF A VORTEX

NOTES

chemical engineering
fluid mechanics
Author

Tom Savage

Published

December 4, 2023

Coherent Structures (CS) in turbulent flows are now commonly defined as vortices.

Therefore, we require an objective definition of a vortex in order to explain coherent structures.

Proposal: define a vortex by the eigenvalues of \(\mathbf{S}^2 + \Omega^2\); where \(\mathbf{S}\) and \(\Omega\) are the symmetric and antisymettric parts of the velocity gradient tensor \(\nabla \mathbf{u}\).

Captures the pressure minimum in a plane perpendicular to the vortex axis.

Turbulent shear flows are dominated by coherent structures.

Coherent Structures are spatially coherent, temporally evolving vortical motions.

Understanding:

  • Entrainment
  • Mixing
  • Heat / Mass Transfer
  • Chemical Reaction / Combustion
  • Drag
  • Aerodynamic Noise Generation
  • Turbulence

Requires an understanding of coherent structures, and therefore vortex dynamics.

Vortex intuition throughout time.

1945: a tube whose surface consists of vortex lines.

  • All vortices are vortex tubes, but not all vortex tubes are vortices (i.e. within a laminar flow pipe).

1979: a multitude of material particles rotating around a common center.

1988: a region containing both a postive second invariant of \(\nabla \mathbf{u}\) and low pressure.

1990: a region of complex eigenvalues of \(\nabla \mathbf{u}\).

The size of a vortex in a viscous fluid depends on the identifier’s threshold, therefore this study is limited to vortex cores.

Vortex core requirements:

  1. Must have net vorticity \(\rightarrow\) net circulation. i.e. fluid is swirling continuously within the vortex core and potential flow regions (inflow or outflow) are therefore excluded. A potential vortex has zero-cross section (i.e. the middle of the vortex?).

  2. Geometry should be Galilean invariant. i.e. regardless of how ‘fast time is going’ the vortex core should remain the same shape.

Inadequacies of intuitive measures

  1. Pressure minimum
  • In 3D pressure may be minimised at a point, or a plane.
  • Able to derive irrotational flows that have minimum pressure planes despite having no vorticity or swirl.

\[u_r = -a(t)r, \; u_z = 2a(t)z, \; u_{\theta} = 0\]

  • In Stokes flow at low \(Re\), pressure gradient is balanced by the viscosity term. Vortices can be formed, whilst pressure is defined only by viscosity (i.e. constant with no minima).
  • Isopressure surfaces do not correspond with vortical surfaces due to scale difference.
  1. Closed or spiralling streamlines and pathlines
  • Pathlines do not satisfy Galilean invariance.
    • Two or more vortices at different speeds in the same reference frame would be obscured.
  • A particle may not complete a full revolution of the vortex centre during the lifetime of a vortex.
  1. Isovorticity surfaces
  • Vorticity sheets are not a vortex despite having large vorticity.
  • If the background shear is comparable to vorcitiy magnitude vortex cores can be missed.

Previously proposed definitions (satisfying Galilean-invarance)

  1. Complex eigenvalues (\(\sigma\)) of the velocity gradient tensor (\(\nabla\mathbf{u}\)).

Complex eigenvalues imply that the local streamline pattern is closed or spirals within a reference frame moving with a point.
